</p><div>If you have never been exposed to software software system design challenges, you might be totally lost on even where to begin. I believe in finding the limits to a certain extend first and then start getting your hands dirty. The way you can start this is by finding some interesting product or services (ideally you are a fan of), and learning about their implementations. You will be surprised that how simple they may look, they most probably involve great deal of complexity. Don’t forget:&nbsp;<a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=k9Zbuuo51go">simple is usually complex</a>&nbsp;and that’s OK™.</div><div><br></div><div><img src="https://tugberkugurlu.blob.core.windows.net/bloggyimages/20190223142605-178ec251-75c5-4fcc-a9de-ad09e6551073-isaac-smith-1182056-unsplash.jpg"></div><!--?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?-->

<div>I believe the biggest suggestion I can give you while approaching to system design challenges is this: not to assume anything! You should pin down the facts and expectations from this system first. Some good questions to ask here are which will help you start this process:</div><div><br></div><!--?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?-->

<ul><li>What is the problem you are trying to solve?&nbsp;</li><li>What is the the peak&nbsp;volume of users that will interact with your system?</li><li>What are&nbsp;the data write and read patterns going to be?</li><li>What are the expected failure cases, how do you plan to mitigate them?</li><li>What are the availability and consistency expectations?</li><li>Do you need to worry about any auditing, regulation aspects?</li><li>What type of sensitive data are you going to be storing?</li></ul><div>These are just a questions few that have worked for me and the teams that I worked with over the years. Once you have answers to these questions (or any other which are relevant to the context you are in), then you should be starting to dive into the technical side of the problem.</div>&nbsp;</div><div><h3>Setting Your Baseline</h3><div><div>What do I mean by the baseline here? Well, in this era of software development, most of the problems "can" be solved by already existing techniques and technologies. Knowing these to a certain extend will give you a head start when you are faced with similar problems. Remember, we are writing software to solve business' and our users' problems and the desire is to do that in a most straight-forward and simple way from a user experience point of view. Why do you need to remember this? It could well be your reality that you should solve problems in unique ways as you might be thinking "what's the point of me writing software then if I am here to follow a pattern?". <b>The craft here is in the decision making process to define where to do what</b>. Surely, we may have challenging, unique problems which we can face at certain times. However, if we have our baseline solid, we will surely know whether we should direct our efforts into finding out ways to solve the problems or further understand the depth of it.</div><div><br></div><div><!--?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?-->

<div>I believe I have convinced you at this point now that having a solid knowledge on how some of the exciting systems are architecturally shaped is quite critical for you to progress on having some appreciation on the craft and a solid baseline.</div><div><br></div><!--?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?-->

<div><span style="font-family: -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, &quot;Segoe UI&quot;, Roboto, &quot;Helvetica Neue&quot;, Arial, sans-serif, &quot;Apple Color Emoji&quot;, &quot;Segoe UI Emoji&quot;, &quot;Segoe UI Symbol&quot;, &quot;Noto Color Emoji&quot;; font-size: 1rem;">Systems are composed of various components, which can be different processes living inside the same physical node or different machines sitting at the separate parts in your network. Some of these resources might be private within your network but some needs to be accessed publicly by your consumers.</span></div><div><span style="font-family: -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, &quot;Segoe UI&quot;, Roboto, &quot;Helvetica Neue&quot;, Arial, sans-serif, &quot;Apple Color Emoji&quot;, &quot;Segoe UI Emoji&quot;, &quot;Segoe UI Symbol&quot;, &quot;Noto Color Emoji&quot;; font-size: 1rem;"><br></span></div><div><!--?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?-->

<div>These resources needs to be able to communicate between them and to the outside world. In context of system design, this again introduces another set of unique challenges. <div>When dealing with communication to the outside world,&nbsp;<a href="https://github.com/donnemartin/system-design-primer/#security">security</a>&nbsp;is always another side-effect that you need to be aware of and actively deal with.&nbsp;</div><div><br></div><div><h3>Connection Distribution</h3><div><!--?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?-->

<blockquote class="blockquote">I am not sure if this logical grouping makes sense here. I will go with it anyway since it’s the closest term that reflects what I want to cover here. </blockquote><div>Systems are formed by gluing multiple components together,&nbsp;and how they communicate with each other often is designed through well-established protocols such as TCP and UDP. However, these protocols are often not enough on their own to cover the needs of today’s systems which can have high load and demands from our consumers. We often need ways to be able to distribute connections in order to handle the high load of our system.</div><div><br></div><div><!--?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?-->

<div><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_Name_System">Domain Name System (DNS)</a>&nbsp;sits at the core of this distribution. A DNS&nbsp;translates a domain name such as <a href="http://www.example.com">www.example.com</a> to an IP address. Besides this, some DNS services can route traffic through various methods such as weighted round robin and latency-based to help distribute the load.</div><div><br></div><!--?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?-->

<div><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Load_balancing_(computing)" style="background-color: rgb(255, 255, 255); font-family: -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, &quot;Segoe UI&quot;, Roboto, &quot;Helvetica Neue&quot;, Arial, sans-serif, &quot;Apple Color Emoji&quot;, &quot;Segoe UI Emoji&quot;, &quot;Segoe UI Symbol&quot;, &quot;Noto Color Emoji&quot;; font-size: 1rem;">Load balancing</a><span style="font-family: -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, &quot;Segoe UI&quot;, Roboto, &quot;Helvetica Neue&quot;, Arial, sans-serif, &quot;Apple Color Emoji&quot;, &quot;Segoe UI Emoji&quot;, &quot;Segoe UI Symbol&quot;, &quot;Noto Color Emoji&quot;; font-size: 1rem;">&nbsp;is very vital and nearly every major system on the Web we interact with today sits behind one or multiple load balancers.&nbsp;Load balancers help us distribute incoming client requests to multiple instances of resources. Structuring Business Logic, Workflows and Components</h3></div><div><div>Thus far, we talked about all the infrastructure related aspects of a system.
